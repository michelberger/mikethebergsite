Raspberry Pi Gauge Cluster

(project currently unnamed)

If there's one thing I don't like about most cars, it's how the engineers chose to set up the array of gauges that we, the drivers, are forced to look at every time we get behind the wheel. Usually functional, but not typically appealing.

You've probably looked at your car's gauge cluster and thought, "Wow, I wish this didn't look so...blegh." How about a fully customizable, opensource software system for the Raspberry Pi, a $35, low-power, credit card-sized, ARM-Linux based microcontroller with impressive graphics capabilities, using the C++ toolkit, OpenFrameworks?

I've been taught many-a-time through my academic experience to never bite off more than you can chew...especially when it comes to programming! This project involves many stages and a HUGE learning-curve (with some electrical engineering fun), so it will definitely take a while to complete. It is expected to provide a fully customizable graphics interface for nearly any make or model of car (provided you can install it without damaging anything), a GUI for customizing your gauge layout, and a big list of features to help make your gauge exactly what you want it to be! This is a tall order, so expect progress to be slooow.

Details about how it will connect to your car are still up in the air. It may connect directly to your instrument cluster's wiring harness (hence the mention of electrical engineering), or I may figure out a way to connect it via your vehicle's OBD-II data-transfer port. Maybe even a mix of the two! Either way will have its gains and drawbacks. For example, connecting it directly to your old instrument cluster's wiring harness may give you:

The Good: The Bad:
  • The quickest readouts (limited to how often the RasPi performs sensor polling)
  • The smoothest gauge movements
  • The most amount of customizable information (SES, SRS, ABS, etc.)
  • The best possible integration
  • A LOT of custom wiring will need to be done
  • Limited to what the old instrument cluster could read
  • A permanently disconnected instrument cluster
  • Potential damage to your vehicle's wiring harness (this part is on YOU!)
  • Possibility for odometer tampering
  • Warning lights on some newer cars, triggered by removing factory instrument cluster
  • Uninterperetable data streams accross some wires


Connecting it to your vehicle's supplied OBD-II port may give you:

The Good: The Bad:
  • Potential for simple Plug-and-Play compatibility
  • Potential for a wide array of input parameters
  • Possible odometer recovery (however, this is unlikely)
  • Possibility for other OBD-II related features (diagnostics, etc.) built in
  • May not be compatible with some OBD-II compliant vehicles
  • Purchase of a separate OBD-II connection dongle
  • Incompatibilities with older vehicles using OBD and OBD-I standard
  • Display lag and other gittery lame-ness
  • Inability to read SRS and ABS fault state on most (if not all) vehicles

Note: Installing this gauge cluster may yield legal ramifications, as there is no sure-fire way to synchronize the associated display that will replace your gauge cluster with the car's actual odometer.
